# Chapter 11: America Enters the War

## A Neutral Nation Watches the Conflict

When World War I erupted in 1914, the **United States** chose neutrality.

Many Americans believed the war was fundamentally a European problem.

The United States was separated from Europe by the Atlantic Ocean and had traditionally avoided becoming deeply involved in European conflicts.

President Woodrow Wilson urged Americans to remain impartial.

The country contained millions of immigrants whose families originated from nations on both sides of the conflict.

Maintaining neutrality seemed the safest course.

For nearly three years, the United States remained outside the war.

Yet neutrality did not mean isolation.

American banks, businesses, and industries traded extensively with the Allied powers.

As the conflict continued, economic and political ties between the United States and the Allies grew stronger.

Events at sea would eventually make neutrality increasingly difficult to maintain.

---

## The Atlantic Becomes a Battlefield

Britain's powerful navy imposed a blockade on Germany.

The blockade aimed to prevent food, raw materials, and military supplies from reaching German ports.

Over time, the blockade contributed to shortages within Germany.

German leaders sought ways to counter British naval superiority.

They turned to submarines.

German **U-boats** could attack merchant vessels transporting goods across the Atlantic.

Unlike traditional warships, submarines could strike unexpectedly and disappear beneath the surface.

Germany hoped submarine warfare would weaken Britain by cutting off essential supplies.

The strategy showed promise.

It also created serious diplomatic problems.

---

## The Challenge of Neutral Shipping

The Atlantic Ocean carried ships from many countries, including neutral nations.

American merchant vessels continued operating throughout the conflict.

When German submarines targeted shipping, they risked harming neutral citizens and businesses.

International law traditionally required warships to stop and search merchant vessels before attacking them.

Submarines faced a different reality.

If a U-boat surfaced, it became vulnerable to attack.

German commanders increasingly favored surprise attacks using torpedoes.

This approach increased effectiveness but also increased the likelihood of civilian casualties.

Tensions between Germany and the United States began to rise.

---

## The Sinking of the Lusitania

One of the most important incidents occurred on **May 7, 1915**.

The British passenger ship **RMS Lusitania** was crossing the Atlantic when it was torpedoed by a German U-boat.

The vessel sank rapidly.

Nearly 1,200 people lost their lives.

Among the dead were 128 Americans.

News of the disaster spread quickly.

American newspapers published dramatic accounts of the sinking.

Public outrage intensified.

Many Americans questioned whether Germany had violated basic principles of humanity.

German officials argued that the ship carried war materials and therefore represented a legitimate target.

The debate continued, but the incident significantly damaged Germany's reputation in the United States.

Although America did not enter the war immediately, relations between the two countries became increasingly strained.

---

## Germany Temporarily Backs Down

Following strong diplomatic protests from President Wilson's administration, Germany moderated its submarine campaign.

German leaders understood that provoking the United States could have serious consequences.

For a time, they attempted to avoid incidents involving American citizens.

This temporary restraint helped preserve peace.

However, the underlying strategic problem remained unresolved.

Germany still needed a way to challenge British naval power.

As the war dragged on, pressure mounted within Germany to adopt more aggressive measures.

---

## The War Changes America

Even while remaining neutral, the United States was becoming increasingly connected to the conflict.

American factories produced enormous quantities of:

* Food
* Clothing
* Ammunition
* Industrial goods

Many of these products were sold to Allied nations.

The American economy expanded rapidly.

Banks provided large loans to Britain and France.

By 1917, an Allied defeat could have significant economic consequences for the United States.

Although economic interests alone did not determine policy, they influenced public discussion.

America's relationship with the Allies became deeper as the war continued.

---

## Unrestricted Submarine Warfare Returns

In early 1917, German leaders made a critical decision.

They resumed **unrestricted submarine warfare**.

Under this policy, German U-boats would attack virtually any vessel entering designated war zones around Britain.

German commanders believed the strategy could force Britain to surrender before American intervention became significant.

It was a risky gamble.

German officials understood that unrestricted submarine warfare might bring the United States into the war.

Nevertheless, they concluded that victory against Britain was worth the risk.

The decision proved fateful.

American ships soon came under threat.

Public opinion shifted further against Germany.

Yet another event would soon make matters even worse.

---

## The Zimmermann Telegram

In January 1917, German Foreign Secretary **Arthur Zimmermann** sent a secret message to Germany's ambassador in Mexico.

The telegram proposed an extraordinary arrangement.

If the United States entered the war against Germany, Mexico would be encouraged to join Germany's side.

In return, Germany promised support for recovering territories lost to the United States during the nineteenth century, including:

* Texas
* New Mexico
* Arizona

British intelligence intercepted the message.

After carefully verifying its authenticity, British officials shared it with the United States.

When the telegram became public, Americans reacted with anger and disbelief.

The idea of a foreign power encouraging conflict on North American soil shocked many citizens.

The Zimmermann Telegram severely damaged Germany's standing in American public opinion.

---

## President Wilson's Decision

By early 1917, several developments had transformed the situation:

* Unrestricted submarine warfare threatened American shipping.
* The Zimmermann Telegram created outrage.
* Economic ties with the Allies remained strong.
* German actions appeared increasingly hostile.

President Wilson initially hoped to avoid war.

However, he gradually concluded that neutrality was no longer sustainable.

On April 2, 1917, Wilson addressed Congress.

He argued that Germany's actions threatened international law and global stability.

One of the most famous lines from his speech declared that:

> "The world must be made safe for democracy."

Wilson presented the conflict as a struggle involving fundamental principles rather than merely national interests.

---

## America Declares War

On **April 6, 1917**, the United States formally declared war on Germany.

The decision marked a turning point in World War I.

Although American military forces were initially limited, the country's long-term potential was enormous.

The United States possessed:

* A large population
* A powerful economy
* Significant industrial capacity
* Vast natural resources

Germany now faced the prospect of fighting not only Britain, France, and Russia, but also one of the world's fastest-growing industrial powers.

The balance of the war began to shift.

---

## Raising an Army

When America entered the war, its standing army was relatively small compared to European forces.

Rapid expansion became necessary.

The government introduced conscription through the **Selective Service Act**.

Millions of men registered for military service.

Training camps appeared across the country.

New units were organized and equipped.

Preparing a modern army required time.

Weapons had to be manufactured.

Officers had to be trained.

Transportation systems had to be coordinated.

The process was immense, but American mobilization progressed rapidly.

---

## The American Expeditionary Forces

The force sent to Europe became known as the **American Expeditionary Forces (AEF)**.

Its commander was **John J. Pershing**.

Pershing insisted that American troops should fight as an independent force rather than being permanently absorbed into British or French units.

The Allies preferred immediate integration because they urgently needed manpower.

Pershing argued that maintaining an independent American army would maximize long-term effectiveness.

A compromise eventually emerged.

American troops cooperated closely with Allied forces while preserving their own command structure.

---

## The Arrival of American Soldiers

The first American soldiers began arriving in Europe during 1917.

Initially, their numbers were modest.

By 1918, however, the flow increased dramatically.

Fresh troops crossed the Atlantic in growing numbers.

For the exhausted Allies, American arrivals provided hope.

Years of warfare had depleted manpower.

Entire generations of European soldiers had already endured tremendous losses.

American reinforcements helped fill gaps and strengthen morale.

Their presence also signaled that Germany faced a worsening strategic situation.

Time was no longer on Germany's side.

---

## The Home Front Mobilizes

American participation extended beyond soldiers.

Factories expanded production.

Women entered new industrial roles.

Farmers increased agricultural output.

Government agencies coordinated transportation and resource allocation.

War bond campaigns encouraged citizens to support the war financially.

The conflict affected nearly every aspect of American society.

Although the United States entered the war later than the European powers, its mobilization became one of the largest national efforts in its history.

---

## The Psychological Impact on Germany

American entry into the war carried enormous psychological significance.

German leaders recognized the danger.

Even if American troops required time to arrive, the country's industrial and economic power was undeniable.

Germany hoped to achieve victory before American strength could be fully mobilized.

This urgency influenced German strategy during 1918.

The final year of the war would become a race against time.

Germany needed success before American manpower tipped the balance decisively.

---

## A New Phase of the War

The entry of the United States transformed World War I.

What had begun as a European conflict now involved a major power from across the Atlantic.

American resources strengthened the Allied position.

German hopes increasingly depended on achieving a decisive result quickly.

The war was entering its final phase.

Yet before victory could be achieved, one more dramatic year of fighting remained.

The battles of 1918 would determine the fate of nations and empires.
