# Chapter 5: The Guns of August

## The Month That Changed History

The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand shocked Europe, but the continent did not immediately plunge into war.

For several weeks after the attack in Sarajevo, political leaders debated their options. Diplomats exchanged messages. Ambassadors attended meetings. Newspapers speculated about possible outcomes.

Many Europeans believed the crisis would eventually pass.

After all, Europe had experienced diplomatic confrontations before. Previous disputes had been resolved through negotiation, compromise, and international conferences.

Yet the crisis of 1914 was different.

The alliances that divided Europe, the military plans prepared by generals, and the growing mistrust between nations combined to create a situation unlike any the continent had faced before.

During the summer of 1914, Europe moved step by step toward disaster.

No single leader intended to start a world war.

Yet each government made decisions that pushed the continent closer to one.

By the beginning of August, the great powers of Europe would be at war.

---

## Austria-Hungary Decides to Act

In Vienna, the assassination created outrage.

Many Austrian leaders believed that Serbia had become a direct threat to the empire's survival.

For years, Serbian nationalism had challenged Austrian authority in the Balkans.

Now the heir to the throne had been murdered by a Bosnian Serb with connections to nationalist networks.

To many officials, the conclusion seemed obvious.

Serbia had to be punished.

The question was how.

Some leaders advocated caution.

They worried that military action might provoke Russian intervention.

Others argued that failure to act would make Austria-Hungary appear weak.

After receiving Germany's support through the Blank Check, the advocates of a strong response gained influence.

Austria-Hungary began preparing an ultimatum.

---

## The Ultimatum to Serbia

On July 23, 1914, Austria-Hungary delivered its ultimatum to Serbia.

The document contained a series of demands.

Some were reasonable and focused on investigating the assassination.

Others were deliberately severe.

One demand required Austrian officials to participate directly in investigations conducted inside Serbia.

Many Serbian leaders viewed this as a violation of national sovereignty.

The ultimatum gave Serbia only forty-eight hours to respond.

Across Europe, diplomats immediately recognized the seriousness of the situation.

The demands appeared designed not merely to seek justice but to create conditions under which Serbia would likely reject them.

The crisis was entering a dangerous phase.

---

## Serbia's Reply

The Serbian government faced a difficult choice.

Rejecting the ultimatum could lead to war.

Accepting every demand could compromise Serbian independence.

After intense discussion, Serbia responded on July 25.

To the surprise of many observers, Serbian leaders accepted most of Austria-Hungary's demands.

They agreed to suppress anti-Austrian organizations.

They promised cooperation in the investigation.

They offered significant concessions.

However, they rejected certain provisions that they believed infringed upon Serbian sovereignty.

Many diplomats considered the response remarkably conciliatory.

Britain's foreign secretary later remarked that no nation had ever accepted such harsh demands to such an extent.

Austria-Hungary, however, remained dissatisfied.

The empire declared that Serbia's reply was inadequate.

The path toward war continued.

---

## Russia Enters the Crisis

Russia watched events closely.

For decades, Russian leaders had presented themselves as protectors of Slavic peoples.

Serbia occupied a special place in Russian foreign policy.

If Austria-Hungary crushed Serbia, Russian influence in the Balkans would suffer a major setback.

Russian leaders therefore faced their own difficult decision.

Should they risk war to support Serbia?

Or should they allow Austria-Hungary to impose its will?

As the crisis deepened, Russia gradually moved toward military preparation.

Although Russian leaders still hoped for a diplomatic solution, they became increasingly unwilling to abandon Serbia.

The possibility of a wider conflict grew more likely with each passing day.

---

## The Declaration of War

On July 28, 1914, exactly one month after the assassination in Sarajevo, Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia.

The First World War had not yet begun.

At this moment, the conflict remained technically limited to two countries.

Yet few observers believed it would stay that way.

Austria-Hungarian artillery soon began bombarding Serbian positions.

Military operations commenced.

The crisis had crossed an important threshold.

Diplomacy was giving way to war.

---

## Mobilization: The Point of No Return

Modern armies required enormous preparation.

Millions of soldiers had to be called up.

Equipment had to be distributed.

Railway schedules had to be coordinated.

Supplies had to be transported.

Military planners spent years designing detailed mobilization timetables.

These plans were so complex that once they began, stopping them became extremely difficult.

Mobilization therefore carried enormous political significance.

To mobilize was not necessarily to declare war.

However, many governments interpreted mobilization as a sign that war was imminent.

As tensions escalated, military preparations accelerated across Europe.

Each nation feared being caught unprepared if conflict expanded.

The result was a dangerous cycle.

One country's mobilization encouraged another's.

Fear fed fear.

The momentum toward war increased.

---

## Germany's Dilemma

Germany now faced a strategic problem.

Its alliance with Austria-Hungary obligated support.

At the same time, German military planners feared a war against both Russia and France.

For years, German strategists had prepared for precisely such a scenario.

Their solution was known as the **Schlieffen Plan**.

The plan assumed that Russia would mobilize slowly.

Germany would therefore defeat France quickly before turning east against Russia.

The strategy depended on speed.

Any delay could prove disastrous.

When Russia began mobilizing, German leaders became increasingly alarmed.

Military timetables started to dominate political decision-making.

The room for diplomacy narrowed.

---

## France and the Alliance System

France had closely allied itself with Russia.

French leaders understood that if Germany fought Russia, France would likely become involved.

The memory of the Franco-Prussian War remained powerful.

Many French citizens still hoped to recover Alsace-Lorraine.

At the same time, France did not want to appear responsible for starting a war.

French leaders therefore acted cautiously while supporting their Russian ally.

As events unfolded, however, it became clear that neutrality would be difficult to maintain.

The alliance system that had developed over decades was beginning to function exactly as many critics had feared.

A regional dispute was becoming a continental crisis.

---

## Britain Watches and Waits

Britain initially remained uncertain.

Unlike France and Russia, Britain had no automatic obligation to enter the conflict.

Many British politicians hoped to preserve peace.

Public opinion was divided.

Some argued that Britain should remain neutral.

Others believed that German expansion threatened the balance of power in Europe.

As long as the fighting remained limited to Eastern and Central Europe, Britain might stay out.

That situation would soon change dramatically.

---

## Germany Invades Belgium

The Schlieffen Plan required German forces to advance through Belgium in order to attack France.

Military planners believed this route offered the fastest path toward Paris.

The problem was that Belgium was neutral.

Its neutrality had been guaranteed by international treaties.

On August 2 and August 3, Germany demanded passage through Belgian territory.

Belgium refused.

German forces invaded.

The decision transformed the crisis.

What had appeared to some as a regional war now looked like a threat to the international order.

For Britain, the invasion created a moral and strategic challenge.

If Germany could violate Belgian neutrality without consequence, the balance of power in Europe might collapse.

The British government decided to act.

---

## Europe Goes to War

Events now moved with astonishing speed.

* July 28: Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia.
* August 1: Germany declared war on Russia.
* August 3: Germany declared war on France.
* August 4: Britain declared war on Germany.

Within days, Europe's major powers were locked in conflict.

The alliance system had transformed a Balkan crisis into a general European war.

Millions of soldiers received orders to mobilize.

Trains carried troops toward the front.

Families said farewell to sons, husbands, and fathers.

Across Europe, crowds gathered in city squares.

Many believed the war would be short.

Some predicted victory within months.

Few understood what modern industrial warfare would actually look like.

The optimism of August 1914 would soon disappear.

---

## The Illusion of a Short War

At the outbreak of war, enthusiasm was common.

Newspapers celebrated military mobilization.

Volunteers rushed to enlist.

Political leaders promised swift victories.

Many soldiers expected to return home by Christmas.

These expectations reflected memories of earlier conflicts.

Previous European wars had often been relatively brief.

The coming war would be different.

Modern industry allowed nations to equip enormous armies.

Railways moved troops on an unprecedented scale.

Machine guns and heavy artillery increased battlefield lethality.

The war that began in August 1914 would last more than four years.

It would kill millions.

It would destroy empires.

And it would reshape the modern world.
