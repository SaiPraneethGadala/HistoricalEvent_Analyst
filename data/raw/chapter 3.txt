# Chapter 3: Nationalism and the Balkans

## The Powder Keg of Europe

In the years before World War I, no region in Europe worried diplomats more than the Balkans.

Located in southeastern Europe between the Adriatic Sea, the Black Sea, and the Mediterranean, the Balkans were a crossroads of cultures, religions, and empires. For centuries, powerful states had competed for influence in the region. Empires rose and fell there. Armies marched across its mountains and valleys. Different peoples lived side by side, often sharing the same towns while speaking different languages and following different traditions.

By the beginning of the twentieth century, the Balkans had become one of the most politically unstable regions in the world.

European statesmen increasingly referred to it as the **"Powder Keg of Europe."**

The nickname reflected a growing fear that a single spark could ignite a much larger explosion.

That fear would eventually prove correct.

---

## Understanding Nationalism

To understand why the Balkans became so dangerous, it is first necessary to understand the powerful force of nationalism.

Nationalism is the belief that people who share a common language, culture, history, or ethnic identity should govern themselves as a nation.

During the nineteenth century, nationalism transformed Europe.

It helped create unified states such as Germany and Italy.

It inspired movements seeking independence from empires.

It encouraged people to think of themselves not merely as subjects of kings or emperors, but as members of nations.

For many people, nationalism was a source of pride and unity.

For empires governing diverse populations, however, nationalism represented a serious challenge.

When ethnic groups demanded self-government, imperial authorities often viewed those demands as threats to stability and territorial integrity.

Nowhere was this conflict more intense than in the Balkans.

---

## The Decline of the Ottoman Empire

For centuries, much of the Balkans had been ruled by the Ottoman Empire.

At its height, the Ottoman state controlled vast territories across southeastern Europe, the Middle East, and North Africa.

By the late nineteenth century, however, the empire was weakening.

Military defeats, economic difficulties, administrative problems, and nationalist uprisings gradually reduced Ottoman power.

As Ottoman influence declined, new states emerged in the Balkans.

Countries such as:

* Serbia
* Bulgaria
* Greece
* Montenegro
* Romania

gained independence or increased autonomy.

Each success inspired other nationalist movements.

Communities that had once lived under Ottoman rule now dreamed of governing themselves.

Yet independence did not solve every problem.

Many ethnic groups remained divided by political borders.

As a result, nationalist ambitions continued to grow.

---

## Serbia and the Dream of South Slav Unity

Among the Balkan states, Serbia became one of the most influential champions of nationalism.

Serbian leaders envisioned a future in which all South Slavic peoples would be united within a single political entity.

This idea became known as **Pan-Slavism**.

Supporters believed that Serbs, Croats, Slovenes, Bosnians, and other Slavic groups shared common cultural and historical roots.

Many argued that these peoples should live within one state rather than under foreign rule.

To Serbian nationalists, this goal represented self-determination.

To neighboring empires, it represented a threat.

Austria-Hungary was especially concerned.

Large numbers of South Slavs lived inside the Austro-Hungarian Empire.

If nationalist movements encouraged these populations to seek independence, the empire's stability could be severely weakened.

As Serbian nationalism grew stronger, tensions between Serbia and Austria-Hungary increased.

---

## Austria-Hungary's Growing Anxiety

Austria-Hungary was one of Europe's most diverse states.

Its population included:

* Germans
* Hungarians
* Czechs
* Slovaks
* Croats
* Serbs
* Romanians
* Ukrainians
* Poles

and many other groups.

Keeping such a diverse empire united required careful political management.

Nationalism threatened that unity.

Imperial leaders feared that if one group successfully achieved independence, others might follow.

The empire could begin to unravel.

For this reason, Austrian officials closely monitored nationalist activity throughout the Balkans.

Particularly troubling was the growth of Serbian influence.

Many Austrian leaders viewed Serbia not merely as a neighboring country but as a potential catalyst for separatist movements within the empire itself.

Relations between the two countries became increasingly hostile.

---

## Russia's Role in the Balkans

Russia also played an important role in Balkan politics.

Russian leaders considered themselves protectors of Slavic peoples throughout Eastern Europe and the Balkans.

This position reflected several factors.

First, Russia shared cultural and religious ties with many Balkan Slavs.

Second, supporting Slavic nationalism increased Russian influence in the region.

Third, Russia sought greater access to important waterways and strategic routes.

As a result, Russia frequently supported Serbia in diplomatic disputes.

This support encouraged Serbian confidence.

It also intensified Austrian fears.

Any conflict between Austria-Hungary and Serbia now carried the risk of Russian involvement.

The Balkans were becoming entangled in the larger rivalries between Europe's great powers.

---

## The Bosnian Crisis of 1908

One of the most significant prewar confrontations occurred in 1908.

For decades, Austria-Hungary had administered Bosnia and Herzegovina, although these territories technically remained under Ottoman sovereignty.

In 1908, Austria-Hungary formally annexed Bosnia.

The decision shocked many observers.

Serbia strongly opposed the annexation.

Bosnia contained a large Slavic population, including many ethnic Serbs.

Serbian nationalists believed the region should eventually become part of a greater Serbian state.

Russia also objected to Austria's actions.

However, Russia was not prepared for military confrontation at that time.

Germany supported Austria-Hungary, forcing Russia and Serbia to back down.

The crisis ended without war.

Nevertheless, it left deep resentment.

Serbian nationalists became increasingly determined to challenge Austrian influence.

Austrian leaders became increasingly suspicious of Serbia.

Trust between the two sides deteriorated further.

---

## The Balkan Wars

Only a few years later, the Balkans experienced another period of instability.

### The First Balkan War (1912)

Several Balkan states formed an alliance against the Ottoman Empire.

Their objective was to seize remaining Ottoman territories in Europe.

The alliance achieved rapid success.

Ottoman forces suffered major defeats.

Large areas changed hands.

The war significantly reduced Ottoman influence in Europe.

### The Second Balkan War (1913)

Victory created new disputes.

Former allies disagreed over how conquered territories should be divided.

Fighting soon erupted among the Balkan states themselves.

The conflict was short but intense.

When it ended, Serbia emerged stronger and more confident than before.

This development alarmed Austria-Hungary.

A larger and more powerful Serbia appeared increasingly capable of challenging imperial authority in the region.

---

## Secret Societies and Revolutionary Nationalism

Nationalism in the Balkans was not limited to politicians and governments.

Various secret organizations also emerged.

Some groups promoted cultural unity and political reform.

Others embraced more radical methods.

Among the most famous was the **Black Hand**, a Serbian nationalist organization founded by military officers.

The group's members believed revolutionary action might be necessary to achieve national goals.

They supported efforts to unite South Slavic peoples and weaken Austro-Hungarian control over Slavic territories.

Although not all Serbian officials supported such organizations, their activities heightened Austrian fears.

Imperial authorities increasingly believed that Serbia posed a direct threat to the stability of Austria-Hungary.

This perception would heavily influence future decisions.

---

## A Region Ready to Explode

By 1914, the Balkans had become one of the most dangerous regions on Earth.

Several powerful forces were interacting simultaneously:

* Rising nationalism
* Imperial decline
* Ethnic tensions
* Territorial disputes
* Great-power rivalries
* Military competition

Each crisis seemed to increase hostility.

Each diplomatic confrontation deepened mistrust.

Yet many European leaders still believed that major war could be avoided.

They assumed diplomacy would continue to contain regional disputes.

What they underestimated was how interconnected the European political system had become.

The alliances that linked the major powers meant that even a local conflict could trigger a much larger confrontation.

The Balkans were no longer merely a regional problem.

They had become a potential trigger for a continental war.

---

## The Final Months Before Disaster

During the first half of 1914, Europe remained at peace.

Factories continued operating.

Governments debated budgets and policies.

Citizens traveled, worked, and planned for the future.

Few imagined that the world stood on the edge of catastrophe.

Yet beneath the surface, tensions remained unresolved.

Austria-Hungary feared Serbian nationalism.

Serbia dreamed of expansion and unification.

Russia supported Slavic interests.

Germany supported Austria-Hungary.

France remained aligned with Russia.

Britain watched developments with increasing concern.

The alliance system was in place.

Military plans had been prepared.

Nationalist passions were running high.

All that remained was a spark.

That spark arrived on a summer day in Sarajevo.

On June 28, 1914, a young nationalist named Gavrilo Princip stepped forward and fired two shots.

Within weeks, Europe would be at war.

Within months, millions of soldiers would be fighting across the continent.

The age of relative peace was about to end.
