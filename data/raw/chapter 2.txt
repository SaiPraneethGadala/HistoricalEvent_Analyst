# Chapter 2: Empires, Alliances, and Rivalries

## A Continent Divided by Ambition

As the twentieth century began, Europe appeared peaceful on the surface. Diplomats attended conferences, monarchs exchanged visits, and international trade reached unprecedented levels. To many citizens, war between the major powers seemed unlikely.

Yet beneath this appearance of stability, Europe was becoming increasingly divided.

The continent was dominated by powerful states, each pursuing its own interests and ambitions. Every government sought security, influence, and prestige. Every nation feared being left behind in a rapidly changing world.

The result was a complicated web of rivalries that connected nearly every major power.

These rivalries did not emerge overnight. They developed over decades through competition for territory, colonies, military strength, and political influence.

By 1914, Europe had become a continent where almost every nation viewed at least one other nation as a potential threat.

The stage was being set for a crisis that would eventually engulf the world.

---

## The German Empire's Search for a Place in the Sun

Germany's unification in 1871 dramatically altered the balance of power in Europe.

Before unification, the German states had been divided and relatively weak compared to Britain, France, and Russia. After unification, however, Germany quickly became one of the most powerful nations on Earth.

Its population exceeded sixty million people.

Its factories produced enormous quantities of steel and manufactured goods.

Its universities led the world in science and engineering.

Its army was widely regarded as the most effective military force in Europe.

German leaders believed that such a powerful nation deserved greater influence in world affairs.

Under Kaiser Wilhelm II, Germany adopted a policy known as *Weltpolitik*—"world policy."

The goal was simple: transform Germany into a global power equal to Britain and France.

This meant acquiring colonies, building a powerful navy, and expanding German influence overseas.

Many Germans supported these ambitions.

They believed Germany had arrived late to the competition for colonies and deserved a larger share of global power.

However, Germany's rise alarmed other nations.

The stronger Germany became, the more its neighbors worried about their own security.

---

## Britain and the Protection of Empire

No country watched Germany's rise more carefully than Britain.

At the beginning of the twentieth century, Britain possessed the largest empire in human history.

Its colonies stretched from Canada to India, from Australia to large portions of Africa.

British trade connected every continent.

The Royal Navy protected shipping lanes and ensured British dominance at sea.

For generations, British security had depended on maintaining naval superiority.

As long as Britain controlled the seas, it could defend its empire and protect its economy.

Germany's decision to build a modern navy therefore caused serious concern in London.

Beginning in the late nineteenth century, Germany launched an ambitious naval construction program.

New battleships rolled out of German shipyards each year.

British leaders feared that Germany intended to challenge their maritime dominance.

An expensive naval arms race began.

Each side built larger and more powerful warships.

The competition consumed enormous resources and increased suspicion between the two countries.

Although Britain and Germany never fought before 1914, their relationship became increasingly strained.

---

## France's Desire for Security

France had its own reasons to fear Germany.

The memory of the Franco-Prussian War remained fresh in French society.

In 1871, France had suffered a humiliating defeat.

Germany had annexed the regions of Alsace and Lorraine, territories that many French citizens considered rightfully French.

The loss became a national trauma.

French schoolchildren learned about the provinces that had been taken.

Politicians spoke openly about recovering them someday.

Military planners prepared for the possibility of another conflict with Germany.

At the same time, France sought allies who could help counterbalance German power.

French leaders understood that Germany's growing population and industrial strength gave it significant advantages.

Alone, France might struggle to resist a German attack.

With allies, the situation would be different.

This search for security would eventually play a major role in the formation of opposing alliance systems.

---

## Russia and the Balkans

To the east, Russia viewed itself as the protector of Slavic peoples throughout Europe.

This belief drew Russia into the complex politics of the Balkans.

The Balkan Peninsula was one of the most unstable regions in Europe.

Located between the Ottoman Empire, Austria-Hungary, and Russia, it was home to numerous ethnic groups, languages, religions, and national movements.

Many Balkan peoples wanted independence.

Others hoped to unite with neighboring states that shared their ethnic identity.

Russia supported several Slavic nationalist movements.

Partly this reflected cultural and religious ties.

Partly it reflected strategic interests.

Russian leaders wanted greater influence in southeastern Europe and access to warm-water ports.

Austria-Hungary viewed these ambitions as a direct threat.

As Russian influence expanded in the Balkans, tensions between the two empires increased.

The region gradually became one of the most dangerous areas in the world.

Diplomats would later refer to it as the "Powder Keg of Europe."

---

## Austria-Hungary: An Empire Under Pressure

Unlike Germany, Austria-Hungary faced serious internal challenges.

The empire governed a remarkably diverse population.

Germans, Hungarians, Czechs, Slovaks, Serbs, Croats, Romanians, Ukrainians, and many others lived within its borders.

Each group possessed its own language, culture, and political aspirations.

Nationalist movements became increasingly active.

Some demanded greater autonomy.

Others sought complete independence.

Still others hoped to join neighboring countries that shared their ethnic identity.

These movements threatened the unity of the empire.

Austrian leaders worried particularly about Serbian nationalism.

Serbia promoted the idea of uniting South Slavic peoples into a larger state.

Such a project could encourage Slavic populations inside Austria-Hungary to rebel.

As a result, Austria-Hungary viewed Serbia as a growing danger.

This rivalry would eventually contribute directly to the outbreak of war.

---

## The Formation of Rival Alliances

European governments increasingly believed that alliances were necessary for survival.

No major power wanted to face a potential enemy alone.

As a result, two large alliance systems gradually emerged.

### The Triple Alliance

Formed in 1882, the Triple Alliance included:

* Germany
* Austria-Hungary
* Italy

The alliance promised mutual support under specific circumstances.

Germany regarded the alliance as protection against France and Russia.

Austria-Hungary viewed it as insurance against external threats.

Italy hoped membership would enhance its security and international status.

### The Triple Entente

Opposing the Triple Alliance was the Triple Entente:

* Britain
* France
* Russia

Unlike the Triple Alliance, the Entente was not initially a formal military alliance.

Instead, it developed through a series of agreements and diplomatic understandings.

Nevertheless, by 1914 the three countries increasingly cooperated on military planning and foreign policy.

Europe had effectively divided into two rival camps.

---

## Why Alliances Made War More Dangerous

Many leaders believed alliances would prevent war.

The logic seemed straightforward.

If potential enemies knew they would face multiple powerful nations, they would hesitate before taking aggressive action.

In practice, however, alliances often had the opposite effect.

They transformed local disputes into international crises.

A conflict involving two countries could quickly draw in several others.

Military planners developed detailed mobilization schedules that depended on rapid action.

Once mobilization began, political leaders found it difficult to stop.

Diplomatic flexibility decreased.

Fear increased.

Governments worried that any delay might place them at a strategic disadvantage.

The alliance system therefore created conditions in which a small crisis could escalate into a continental war.

---

## An Era of Growing Suspicion

By the early twentieth century, trust between Europe's major powers was steadily declining.

Germany worried about encirclement by France and Russia.

France feared German aggression.

Britain monitored German naval expansion.

Russia competed with Austria-Hungary for influence in the Balkans.

Austria-Hungary feared nationalist movements supported by Serbia and Russia.

Each government believed it was acting defensively.

Each believed its actions were necessary for national security.

Yet these defensive measures often appeared threatening to others.

The result was a cycle of suspicion.

Military spending increased.

Diplomatic disputes multiplied.

Nationalist rhetoric became more intense.

The peace of Europe remained intact, but it rested upon increasingly fragile foundations.

---

## The World Moves Toward Crisis

As 1914 approached, Europe resembled a vast structure held together by careful diplomacy and mutual caution.

Most political leaders did not want a general war.

Most citizens expected peace to continue.

Trade remained strong.

Cities continued to grow.

Scientific progress inspired optimism.

Yet the forces driving Europe toward conflict had not disappeared.

They were becoming stronger.

Empires competed for influence.

Nationalist movements demanded change.

Military leaders prepared for future wars.

Alliance commitments linked distant nations together.

Europe's leaders believed they could manage these tensions.

Soon they would discover how difficult that task had become.

Only one spark remained necessary.

And that spark would emerge in the Balkans.
