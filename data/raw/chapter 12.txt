# Chapter 12: The Russian Revolution and the Collapse of the Eastern Front

## An Empire Under Strain

When World War I began in 1914, the **Russian Empire** was one of the largest nations on Earth.

Stretching across Europe and Asia, it possessed vast territory, enormous natural resources, and a population exceeding 170 million people.

Yet beneath this impressive appearance lay serious weaknesses.

Russia remained less industrialized than many Western European nations.

Transportation networks were underdeveloped.

Large portions of the population lived in poverty.

Political dissatisfaction simmered beneath the surface.

The war would expose and intensify these problems.

By 1917, the Russian Empire would collapse entirely.

Its fall would become one of the most significant events of the twentieth century.

---

## Russia Enters the War

At the outbreak of World War I, many Russians greeted the conflict with enthusiasm.

Patriotic demonstrations filled city streets.

Citizens rallied behind **Tsar Nicholas II** and the imperial government.

Russia's leaders believed they were defending Serbia and protecting Slavic interests in the Balkans.

Initially, national unity appeared strong.

However, military reality soon challenged this optimism.

The Russian Army was enormous, but size alone could not guarantee success.

Problems quickly emerged.

---

## Early Military Difficulties

Russia mobilized rapidly in 1914 and launched offensives against Germany and Austria-Hungary.

The strategy aimed to relieve pressure on France by forcing Germany to fight on two fronts.

Some operations achieved temporary success.

Others ended disastrously.

One of the most significant defeats occurred during the **Battle of Tannenberg**.

German forces encircled and destroyed a large Russian army.

The defeat shocked Russian commanders and exposed weaknesses in military leadership and communication.

Additional setbacks followed.

Although Russian forces often displayed remarkable courage, they struggled against better-equipped opponents.

Losses mounted steadily.

---

## The Cost of Total War

As the war continued, Russia faced challenges beyond the battlefield.

Modern warfare consumed enormous quantities of resources.

Armies required:

* Rifles
* Ammunition
* Artillery
* Uniforms
* Food
* Medical supplies

Russian industry struggled to meet demand.

Transportation networks became overwhelmed.

Railways carried troops and military equipment while civilians faced shortages.

Economic disruption spread throughout the country.

Prices rose rapidly.

Food became increasingly difficult to obtain.

Public frustration grew.

---

## Life on the Home Front

The war affected ordinary Russians profoundly.

In major cities such as **Petrograd** and **Moscow**, long lines formed outside bakeries and food stores.

Fuel shortages became common.

Workers faced difficult conditions.

Families worried about relatives serving at the front.

Millions of soldiers had been mobilized.

As casualties increased, nearly every community experienced loss.

The longer the war continued, the more public confidence declined.

Many citizens blamed the government for failing to address the crisis.

---

## Tsar Nicholas II Takes Command

In 1915, Tsar Nicholas II made a decision that would have major consequences.

He assumed personal command of the Russian Army.

The move was intended to demonstrate leadership and inspire confidence.

Instead, it linked the Tsar directly to military outcomes.

Whenever the army suffered defeats, public criticism increasingly focused on him.

Meanwhile, government administration remained in the hands of officials in Petrograd.

Political instability worsened.

The Tsar's decision unintentionally weakened the monarchy's position.

---

## The Influence of Rasputin

During the war years, controversy surrounded **Grigori Rasputin**.

Rasputin was a religious mystic who gained influence within the imperial court.

Because he appeared able to help the Tsar's son, who suffered from a serious medical condition, members of the royal family trusted him.

Many Russians viewed Rasputin with suspicion.

Rumors spread regarding his influence over government decisions.

Newspapers and political opponents used his presence as evidence of corruption and incompetence.

Although many stories were exaggerated, the controversy damaged public confidence in the monarchy.

By late 1916, dissatisfaction with the government had reached dangerous levels.

---

## Revolution Begins

In early 1917, conditions deteriorated further.

Food shortages became severe.

Strikes spread across industrial centers.

Workers demanded better wages and improved living conditions.

Protests erupted in Petrograd.

Initially, authorities expected demonstrations to fade.

Instead, they expanded rapidly.

Thousands of people filled the streets.

Soon, soldiers stationed in the city faced a choice.

Should they suppress the protesters?

Or should they join them?

Many chose the latter.

The government's ability to maintain control began collapsing.

---

## The February Revolution

In March 1917 (February according to Russia's old calendar), the Russian monarchy fell.

The event became known as the **February Revolution**.

Faced with widespread unrest and declining support, Tsar Nicholas II abdicated.

His reign ended.

The Romanov dynasty, which had ruled Russia for more than three centuries, came to an end.

Crowds celebrated.

Many Russians believed a new era had begun.

However, removing the Tsar did not solve the country's problems.

War continued.

Economic difficulties remained.

Political divisions deepened.

The revolution was only beginning.

---

## The Provisional Government

After the Tsar's abdication, political leaders established a **Provisional Government**.

Its purpose was to govern Russia until elections could be held.

Many citizens hoped democratic reforms would transform the country.

Yet the new government faced enormous challenges.

Most importantly, it chose to continue fighting World War I.

This decision proved controversial.

Many Russians were exhausted by years of conflict.

Soldiers wanted peace.

Workers wanted economic relief.

Peasants wanted land reform.

The Provisional Government struggled to satisfy these competing demands.

Its popularity declined rapidly.

---

## Lenin Returns

While the Provisional Government struggled, a revolutionary leader prepared to return to Russia.

His name was **Vladimir Lenin**.

Lenin led the **Bolshevik Party**, a radical political organization advocating socialist revolution.

With German assistance, Lenin traveled from exile back to Russia in 1917.

Germany hoped his activities would weaken Russia and reduce its ability to continue fighting.

The strategy would prove effective.

Upon arriving in Petrograd, Lenin issued a clear message.

He called for:

* Peace
* Land redistribution
* Transfer of power to workers' councils

His slogans resonated with many Russians frustrated by war and hardship.

---

## The Bolsheviks Gain Support

Throughout 1917, the Bolsheviks expanded their influence.

The Provisional Government appeared increasingly ineffective.

Military failures continued.

Economic conditions worsened.

Political instability intensified.

Lenin and his supporters argued that only revolutionary change could solve Russia's problems.

Their message attracted workers, soldiers, and some peasants.

By autumn, the Bolsheviks had become a major political force.

The stage was set for another revolution.

---

## The October Revolution

In November 1917 (October according to the old Russian calendar), Bolshevik forces seized key locations in Petrograd.

The event became known as the **October Revolution**.

Government buildings were occupied.

Communication centers were secured.

The Provisional Government collapsed.

Unlike many earlier revolutions, the takeover involved relatively limited fighting in the capital.

The Bolsheviks now controlled the government.

Lenin emerged as the dominant political figure in Russia.

A new era had begun.

---

## Russia Leaves the War

The Bolsheviks had promised peace.

To fulfill that promise, they entered negotiations with Germany.

The talks produced the **Treaty of Brest-Litovsk** in March 1918.

Under the treaty, Russia withdrew from World War I.

The terms were severe.

Russia lost large territories and millions of inhabitants.

Many Bolsheviks considered the agreement humiliating.

Nevertheless, Lenin believed peace was necessary to preserve the revolution.

Russia's participation in World War I came to an end.

---

## The Collapse of the Eastern Front

Russia's withdrawal transformed the strategic situation.

For years, Germany had fought on two major fronts:

* Western Front against Britain and France
* Eastern Front against Russia

With Russia out of the war, German leaders could transfer troops westward.

This development created new opportunities.

Germany hoped to achieve victory before American forces arrived in overwhelming numbers.

The final year of the war would become a race between German offensives and Allied reinforcements.

---

## The Russian Civil War

Although Russia had left World War I, peace did not immediately follow.

The country descended into civil war.

Supporters of the Bolsheviks fought opponents known collectively as the Whites.

The conflict lasted for years and caused immense suffering.

Eventually, the Bolsheviks emerged victorious.

Their success laid the foundation for the future **Soviet Union**.

The Russian Revolution therefore reshaped not only World War I but also global politics for decades to come.

---

## Why the Russian Revolution Matters

The Russian Revolution ranks among the most important events in modern history.

It:

* Ended the Russian Empire
* Removed Russia from World War I
* Brought the Bolsheviks to power
* Inspired revolutionary movements worldwide
* Created the foundations of the Soviet Union

The revolution demonstrated how war could destabilize entire societies.

Military defeats, economic hardship, and political dissatisfaction combined to destroy one of Europe's oldest monarchies.

The consequences would influence global history throughout the twentieth century.
