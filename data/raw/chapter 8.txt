# Chapter 8: Verdun – The Battle of Attrition

## A Battle Meant to Break a Nation

By the beginning of 1916, World War I had become a war of stalemate.

For more than a year, millions of soldiers had faced one another across trenches stretching from the North Sea to Switzerland. Both sides had launched offensives. Both sides had suffered enormous losses. Yet neither side had achieved a decisive breakthrough.

Military leaders searched desperately for a solution.

Among them was **General Erich von Falkenhayn**, Chief of the German General Staff.

Falkenhayn believed that Germany could not win the war through a single dramatic breakthrough. The defenses of the Western Front were simply too strong.

Instead, he developed a different strategy.

If Germany could not destroy the French Army in one battle, perhaps it could force France to bleed itself to death.

His chosen target was a small city in northeastern France.

Its name was **Verdun**.

Within a few months, that name would become synonymous with suffering, endurance, and sacrifice.

---

## Why Verdun?

At first glance, Verdun seemed an unusual choice.

The city was not France's largest population center.

It was not the capital.

It was not a major industrial hub.

Yet Verdun possessed enormous symbolic importance.

For centuries, it had served as one of France's principal fortress cities.

Its defenses protected important routes into the interior of the country.

French military tradition regarded Verdun as a symbol of national strength.

Falkenhayn understood this.

He believed that France would never willingly abandon the city.

If Germany attacked Verdun, French leaders would feel compelled to defend it regardless of the cost.

The battle would become a contest of endurance.

Germany would force France to commit more and more troops.

Eventually, Falkenhayn hoped, French resistance would collapse.

His goal was not necessarily to capture Verdun.

His goal was to destroy the French Army.

---

## The Opening Bombardment

On the morning of **February 21, 1916**, German artillery opened fire.

What followed was one of the largest bombardments the world had ever seen.

Thousands of guns unleashed shells upon French positions.

The ground shook continuously.

Forests disappeared beneath explosions.

Trenches collapsed.

Entire sections of the landscape were transformed into craters.

For many soldiers, it seemed as if the earth itself was being destroyed.

The bombardment lasted for hours.

Then German infantry advanced.

The Battle of Verdun had begun.

---

## The First German Advances

The initial German assault achieved significant success.

French defenses were caught off guard by the scale of the attack.

Several positions fell.

Among the most important was **Fort Douaumont**, the largest fort in the Verdun defensive system.

The fort was captured surprisingly quickly.

Its loss shocked France.

Many observers feared that Verdun itself might soon fall.

German forces continued advancing.

For a time, victory appeared possible.

Yet the French Army refused to retreat.

Instead, reinforcements began arriving in increasing numbers.

The battle was only beginning.

---

## Philippe Pétain Takes Command

At this critical moment, the French government appointed **General Philippe Pétain** to oversee the defense of Verdun.

Pétain quickly recognized the challenge before him.

French troops were exhausted.

German artillery remained overwhelming.

Morale was under severe pressure.

Yet Pétain also understood something important.

If France could maintain a steady flow of men and supplies into Verdun, the city might survive.

He therefore focused on logistics.

Rather than seeking dramatic offensives, he concentrated on sustaining the defense.

This decision would prove crucial.

---

## The Sacred Road

The survival of Verdun depended upon a single supply route.

This road connected the battlefield to the rest of France.

It became known as the **Voie Sacrée**, or **"Sacred Road."**

Day and night, trucks traveled along this route.

They carried:

* Soldiers
* Food
* Ammunition
* Medical supplies

As wounded men moved in one direction, reinforcements moved in the other.

The road became a lifeline.

Without it, Verdun would almost certainly have fallen.

French authorities devoted enormous effort to keeping the route open.

Workers constantly repaired damage.

Traffic operated around the clock.

The Sacred Road became a symbol of national determination.

---

## Life Inside the Inferno

For the soldiers fighting at Verdun, conditions were almost unimaginable.

Artillery dominated every aspect of life.

Shells exploded continuously.

Men spent hours or days inside cramped positions.

Sleep was difficult.

Food often arrived late.

Water was scarce.

The landscape itself seemed hostile.

Trees had been shattered into splinters.

Fields had become fields of craters.

Bodies often remained where they fell because recovery was impossible.

Many soldiers later described Verdun as a vision of hell.

The constant bombardment placed enormous strain on physical and mental health.

Fear became a permanent companion.

Yet the fighting continued.

---

## The Battle Becomes a Symbol

As casualties mounted, Verdun acquired immense symbolic significance.

For France, defending the city became a matter of national honor.

The slogan **"They Shall Not Pass"** spread throughout the country.

Newspapers celebrated resistance.

Political leaders emphasized the importance of holding the line.

Soldiers arriving at Verdun understood they were participating in something larger than a military operation.

They were defending a symbol of France itself.

The battle became a test of national will.

---

## German Difficulties

Although German forces initially advanced, they soon encountered serious problems.

The battlefield made movement difficult.

French resistance stiffened.

German casualties increased steadily.

Most importantly, Germany faced the same challenge that confronted France.

Maintaining a prolonged offensive required enormous resources.

Every advance came at a cost.

Every captured position had to be defended.

Every shell fired had to be replaced.

The longer the battle continued, the more expensive it became.

What Falkenhayn intended as a battle of attrition began affecting both sides.

Germany was bleeding alongside France.

---

## Fort Vaux and Heroic Resistance

One of the most famous episodes of Verdun involved **Fort Vaux**.

German troops attacked the fort repeatedly.

The French garrison fought under desperate conditions.

Water supplies dwindled.

Air quality deteriorated.

Communication became difficult.

Despite these hardships, defenders continued resisting.

Only after exhausting every possibility did the survivors surrender.

Their determination became legendary.

Stories from Fort Vaux spread throughout France and strengthened public morale.

The defense demonstrated the extraordinary resilience displayed by soldiers on both sides.

---

## The Somme Changes Everything

During the summer of 1916, events elsewhere began influencing Verdun.

The British and French launched a major offensive on the **Somme River**.

The Battle of the Somme forced Germany to redirect troops and resources.

Pressure on Verdun gradually eased.

French forces gained opportunities to reorganize and counterattack.

The strategic balance began shifting.

Germany could no longer concentrate entirely on Verdun.

The battle entered a new phase.

---

## The French Counteroffensive

By autumn, French forces had recovered much of their strength.

Artillery support improved.

Fresh troops arrived.

German momentum weakened.

The French launched a series of counterattacks.

Several key positions were recaptured.

Most notably, **Fort Douaumont** returned to French control.

The achievement boosted morale across the country.

Gradually, the immediate threat to Verdun diminished.

After months of continuous fighting, the battle was approaching its conclusion.

---

## The End of the Battle

By December 1916, major operations around Verdun largely ceased.

The battlefield remained devastated.

Entire villages had disappeared.

Forests no longer existed.

The landscape resembled the surface of another planet.

Neither side had achieved a decisive strategic victory.

Germany failed to destroy the French Army.

France successfully defended Verdun but at an enormous cost.

The battle had lasted approximately ten months.

It became one of the longest engagements of the war.

---

## The Human Cost

Verdun's casualty figures remain staggering.

Historians estimate that approximately:

* **300,000 soldiers were killed**
* More than **700,000 casualties occurred overall**

These numbers included both French and German forces.

Many soldiers who survived carried physical and psychological scars for the rest of their lives.

The battle demonstrated the destructive power of industrial warfare.

Modern weapons could inflict casualties on a scale previously unimaginable.

Verdun became a symbol of both courage and tragedy.

---

## Verdun's Legacy

Long after the guns fell silent, Verdun remained central to French memory.

Memorials were constructed.

Cemeteries expanded.

Veterans returned to remember fallen comrades.

The battle became part of France's national identity.

For historians, Verdun represents one of the clearest examples of attrition warfare.

Victory was sought not through maneuver but through exhaustion.

The battle revealed the terrible logic of trench warfare.

When neither side could break through, both sides paid a terrible price.

Verdun taught military leaders important lessons.

Unfortunately, those lessons would not end the war.

Even larger offensives still lay ahead.

Within months, another battle would begin.

It would become one of the bloodiest in British military history.

Its name was **The Somme**.
